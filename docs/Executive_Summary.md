# Executive Summary: Benchmarking & Evaluation Study

## 1. Context & Objective
In modern telecommunications, transmitting digital images securely is a critical challenge. Standard commercial encryption methods (like AES or RSA) are designed for text and are highly inefficient for images due to their large size and the heavy redundancy of pixel values. 

A promising alternative is **chaos-based cryptography**, which encrypts images using the unpredictable, complex patterns generated by chaotic physical systems. Specifically, this project implements a **fractional-order hyperchaotic system**—a highly complex mathematical model with "memory effects" that make its patterns even harder to predict. 

For two devices to securely communicate using chaos, their chaotic generators must be synchronized. We implement **Sliding Mode Control (SMC)**, a robust engineering method, to lock the receiver's chaotic generator onto the transmitter's generator. 

The objective of this study was to build and benchmark a dual-mode system comparing:
1. **Paper Mode**: A faithful realization of academic paper proposals where encryption keys are derived directly from the physical synchronized trajectories.
2. **Robust Mode**: A practical engineering pipeline that extracts bits from the chaos, sanitizes them using a cryptographic hash (SHA-256), and expands them using a Pseudo-Random Number Generator (PRNG).

---

## 2. What Was Built
We constructed a complete, frozen image secure communication testbed including:
* **The Physics Solver**: A mathematically corrected fractional numerical solver (Adams-Bashforth-Moulton) to simulate the chaotic attractor.
* **The Control Engine**: A Finite-Time Projective Sliding Mode Control synchronization link that locks the receiver onto the transmitter.
* **The Cryptographic Ciphers**:
  * **Robust Mode**: Extracts sign bits, hashes them, and expands keys uniformly.
  * **Paper Mode**: Quantizes the states directly into a noise-free coarse key combination.
* **The Benchmark Suite**: An automated metrics engine evaluating the system on standard images (Lena, Baboon, Peppers, Cameraman, House) across security, speed, and noise resistance.

---

## 3. Major Findings

### Cryptographic Security Trade-off
* **Robust Mode** achieves near-perfect security metrics: ideal information entropy (7.98), maximum pixel change rates (NPCR of 99.90%), and uniform key distribution. This completely masks the image structure and resists brute-force attacks.
* **Paper Mode** exhibits an **entropy deficit**. Because direct quantization must be coarse to tolerate synchronization noise, the key sequence collapses to only **17 unique values**, making it highly vulnerable to statistical analysis and decoding.

### The Synchronization Noise Paradox
* **Robust Mode** has a "cliff-edge" collapse. It is highly robust to small noise (up to $\sigma = 5 \times 10^{-3}$) due to a voting filter. However, if the noise exceeds this, a single bit flip in the key string changes the SHA-256 seed entirely, dropping key parity instantly to $0.39\%$ (complete noise) and failing decryption.
* **Paper Mode** degrades gracefully under noise ($99.91\%$ key parity at low noise). However, because the encryption uses a chained diffusion algorithm, even a $0.09\%$ key mismatch corrupts the entire decryption chain. Thus, Paper Mode decryption still fails under active noise.

---

## 4. Final Recommendations

1. **For Real-World Security Deployment**: **Robust Mode** is strongly recommended. It isolates the image cipher from physical noise, ensures uniform key distribution, and resists advanced cryptanalysis.
2. **For Theoretical Academic Publication**: **Paper Mode** is recommended. It demonstrates the direct physical mapping of synchronization trajectories onto secure key spaces, representing the core contribution of projective synchronization theory.
